        How to use Infrared Photography Studio

        Quick start

        1. Open image(s) (or pick an example). RAW — NEF or DNG — unlocks the full color range. Pick several and they become a session: a strip of previews along the bottom, tap to switch, each photo keeping its own edit — held safely, so a closed tab offers to Resume right where you were (masks are the one thing a resume can't restore — they reset with a fresh open). Done (on the strip) ends the session and frees the space.
        2. Quick look a folder (start screen) when you just want to see what's in a set: an instant grid of auto-balanced previews, nothing saved. Tap the keepers and Keep in a session sends them straight into an editable session.
        3. On open, the app automatically sets white balance, exposure and denoise from the photo itself.
        4. Tap foliage in the photo to fine-tune white balance — the classic IR move.
        5. Pick a Look, adjust sliders to taste, then Export & Save.

        Gestures

        • Tap the photo — set white balance from that point (aim at foliage).
        • Pinch — zoom (up to 8×); drag to pan while zoomed.
        • Press & hold the photo (or the Hold: Original button) — compare with the original.

        Looks & adjustments

        Looks are one-tap recipes; every slider stays live afterwards so you can push further. Looks tagged R⇆B use the red⇆blue channel swap — the move that makes IR sky blue and foliage red/gold. Toggle the swap button to see its effect on any look. HIE glow is film-style halation — the HIE B&W look uses it, and the slider layers it onto any other look too.

        Per-color moves the Sky and Foliage color bands independently — e.g. Aerochrome, then Sky hue +40 for a deeper sky without touching the trees. The bands follow the subject through a channel swap (Sky keeps meaning sky); the small text under each box shows the colors it's grabbing right now.

        Which color tool when?

        The color tools stack from broadest to most surgical — reach for the smallest one that does the job:

        • Channel swap — flips the whole color world at once (red⇆blue). The IR move.
        • Hue shift — rotates every color together, as far as you like. Use this for big moves: getting a color family into the right neighborhood.
        • Per-color (Sky / Foliage) — each box owns half the color wheel, and they follow the swap.
        • Color mixer — eight chips for surgery on one color. Use Pick color from photo: tap the button, tap the photo, and the chip owning that exact on-screen color selects itself.

        A mixer chip bends only its own neighborhood on purpose: photos hold smooth color gradients (a sky drifts from cyan to blue), and nearby colors must land near each other or those gradients tear into visible bands. That's why a chip's Hue reaches ±60° and fades toward its neighbors — it's what keeps the adjustment invisible-clean. When one chip's reach isn't enough, make the big move with Hue shift first, then finish with the chip.

        Light & area tools

        • Clarity — punches up local structure (or softens, pulled left). Dehaze — cuts the veil that flattens distant subjects; brightness-only, so colors stay put.
        • Sharpen & Texture (Basic) — Sharpen crisps fine edges; Texture works the mid-size surface structure (left smooths it). Both are luminance-only, so colors never shift.
        • Tone curve + Luminance — the curve shapes blacks through highlights; Luminance lifts or drops the whole image without clipping either end.
        • Masks — radial, gradient, painted brush, tap-a-colour, or automatic sky: local adjustments for one area only. Masks belong to the photo; saved looks stay portable.
        • Dust & spots (Basic) — arm Heal spots and tap a dust mote: it's patched from clean pixels nearby, in the preview and the export alike. Visualize spots makes dust jump out of flat skies; Find spots automatically sweeps the smooth areas, heals what it finds, and rings every fix for review — tap a ring to put that one back. Heals belong to the photo — they never ride in saved looks, profiles or a batch.
        • IR lens fixes — pulls down the center hot-spot IR-converted lenses produce, and corrects (or adds) corner vignette.
        • Crop & straighten (top bar) — drag the box's corners to crop, drag inside it to move it, or drag Straighten to level a horizon (the crop auto-shrinks so no empty corner shows). A view onto the photo, not a re-bake — masks and healed spots stay exactly where they are underneath. Composition-specific: never rides in saved looks, profiles or a batch.

        Profiles

        .cube bakes your color look for Photoshop/video apps. .dcp is a Lightroom camera profile (beta). Denoise, glow, sharpen, texture, clarity, dehaze, masks and heals are spatial effects and can't ride along in profiles.

        Works with

        JPEG/PNG from any camera · HEIC (in Safari) · RAW from any Bayer-sensor camera converted to DNG (free Adobe DNG Converter, or Lightroom's Convert to DNG) · Nikon NEF natively (classic compressed; not High-Efficiency — Z8/Z9 and the Z50 II's HE / HE* files need converting to DNG first) · Fujifilm X-Trans not supported. Tuned on an IR-converted Nikon Z50.

        Batch process — develop a whole set at once

        Open image(s) is for editing (one photo, or a few as a session); Batch process is for output — it develops a whole set unattended and hands you a .zip. Tap it (in the top bar next to Open image(s), or on the start screen) and it first asks what goes on every photo: your current edit (the grade on screen — offered only when a photo is open), a saved look from My looks, a built-in look (Aerochrome, Goldie, B&W IR…), or Auto-balance only — no look at all, just each photo properly white-balanced and exposed, which is the quickest way to preview a whole folder and see what you've got. Then pick the files. Each photo is auto-balanced on its own (its own white balance, exposure, denoise and lens hot-spot fix) with the chosen grade layered on top. The set comes back as one .zip you save in a single tap, at the Format and Resolution set in Export. (White balance is per-photo, so this is for a shoot in one style; the exact frame on screen isn't copied pixel-for-pixel.)

        What rides along, and what doesn't: a batch carries the creative look only. Masks (radial, gradient, brush) and the IR lens fixes sliders — Hot-spot, its size, and Vignette — are tied to one specific frame, so they do not carry into a batch. Instead every photo gets its own hot-spot correction chosen from its EXIF lens; a photo whose lens isn't in the EXIF is processed without that fix, and the finish summary counts how many that was (RAW frames skip it too — the hot-spot profiles are JPEG-only for now).

        Every finished photo is saved to the app's own storage as it completes, so nothing is ever lost: you can stop mid-run and keep what's done, tap Continue to resume the rest, and if the app ever crashes or is closed mid-batch, the start screen offers to recover the finished images next time you open it. If the device runs out of storage mid-batch, it stops the same gentle way — save the .zip to free space, then Continue.

        Install as an app — one, two, or three

        On iPad/iPhone: open the page you want to install, tap Safari's Share button (the square with the arrow), then Add to Home Screen. You get a full-screen app with its own icon that works offline. On Android/Chrome, use the browser's Install app menu entry.

        You're in the installed app right now — it has no Safari Share button, and links can't leave the app either, so Add to Home Screen can't be reached from here. then open Safari, paste it in the address bar, and tap Share → Add to Home Screen there.

        Infrared and Macro Studio are separate tools under one Studio launcher (the ‹ in the top-left goes back to it), and you choose what to install:

        • This tool alone — install from here and its icon opens straight into the Infrared editor, no menu.
        • The whole Studio — install from the ‹ Studio launcher instead: one icon, then pick Infrared or Macro each time.
        • Both, or all three — install any mix; each is independent, so the Infrared app never carries the Macro engine and vice-versa.

        You can add or remove any of them later — installing one never blocks the others.

        Tips

        • Import from Files, not the Photo Library — iOS silently converts RAW to JPEG there. A zipped RAW also works.
        • Scenes with big empty dark skies take more Contrast; scenes with shadow detail (roads, dark trees) look best gentle.
        • 720nm-filter shots are near-monochrome by nature — that's what B&W IR, Sepia IR and HIE B&W are for.
